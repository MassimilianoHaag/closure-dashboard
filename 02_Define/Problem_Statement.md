## Point of View Statement

**Creative professionals who do project-based work need a way to capture and articulate what they made — in the moment they finish it — because the window of highest memory and clarity is also the window of lowest motivation to document, and that window closes fast.**

---

## Unpacked

### Who
Project-based creative professionals — primarily UX and product designers — who ship discrete pieces of work (campaigns, products, features, delivered projects) and are expected to have a portfolio that explains not just *what* they made, but *why* and *how*.

Architects are a plausible secondary audience based on secondary research, but no primary data was collected. Do not build primary decisions around them yet.

### What they need
A way to capture the story of a project — process, decisions, hardest moments — without it requiring a separate act of sitting down and writing.

### Why they can't do it now
Three converging blockers, in order of evidence strength:

1. **Writing is the bottleneck, not gathering or structuring.**
   4 of 5 survey respondents identified writing-related pain as the hardest part — either "writing the actual words" or "articulating process and decisions, not just the final output." Asset gathering ranked last. The problem is verbal, not operational. *(Fully triangulated: primary + secondary.)*

2. **The moment passes before the motivation arrives.**
   Documentation gets deferred because the immediate cost (producing the words) outweighs the felt benefit at the exact moment work ships. Users move on; the memory fades; the documentation never happens. *"I wanted to move on to the next thing."* — R4. *(Primary + secondary: Gamboa framing.)*

3. **Documenting feels like starting a second project on something already finished.**
   The work-of-the-work pattern: re-gathering, re-cutting, re-narrating finished work is a new production run on old material. *"Takes much time documenting each detail."* — R5. *(Partial: present in primary, explicitly named in secondary.)*

### What exists today
Portfolio platforms (Behance, Semplice, Carbonmade) solve presentation — they assume the writing already happened. Cohort programs solve accountability. Nothing catches the moment of completion. The gap is not a better portfolio tool; it's a capture tool that works *before* the portfolio stage begins.

---

## What this problem statement does NOT claim

- **Perfectionism / "it wasn't good enough."** Secondary research is strong on this; primary survey returned silence. Not load-bearing until a follow-up interview surfaces it directly.
- **"No clear done moment" is solved.** 2 of 5 respondents said their work ships gradually, with no discrete completion point. This is a real constraint — the capture trigger mechanic cannot assume an event-fired trigger. The user must decide when "done" is.

---

## The single sharpest version

> *Creative professionals don't document their work because producing the words is harder than doing the work itself — and by the time the motivation arrives, the memory is already gone.*

---

## Open question this statement leaves for Define

**What is the right unit of capture?**
Is the user capturing a whole project, a phase, a key decision, or a milestone? The answer determines what the capture prompt says, how the gallery organizes, and how the portfolio assembles over time. Define must commit to one before wireframing begins.
