# Secondary Research — Portfolio Procrastination

**Owner:** Both
**Status:** Draft — to be refined as we add findings
**Method:** Web search across designer blogs, architecture career guides, and CBT/perfectionism literature
**Date:** 2026-05-21

This document captures secondary research evidence that supports (or challenges) our hypothesis: that creative professionals chronically defer portfolio documentation because the act happens too late, after the memory and emotional investment have decayed.

The research is *secondary* — it's existing writing by other people, not primary interviews. It exists to ground our framing in something more than our intuitions before we run our own interviews.

---

## Core finding: this is a well-documented phenomenon

Multiple credible sources independently describe portfolio procrastination as a near-universal experience for designers. The language is striking — words like *"hate," "dreaded," "notoriously procrastinate," "least favourite task,"* and *"painful task"* appear across unrelated blog posts. This isn't a niche pain we discovered; it's an established problem with a consistent emotional signature.

Notable quotes (paraphrased, attribution preserved):

- Tobias van Schneider (DESK Magazine, 2021) — *"Designers notoriously procrastinate on their portfolio. Even if they have amazing work and dozens of great ideas, they take forever to start or struggle to finish."*
- Tirso Gamboa (2020) — designers "all share the same pain when updating their portfolios" because portfolio documentation "isn't part of [their] design process."
- Creative Bloq (2019) — "designers notoriously procrastinate before launching their portfolio... they want it to be so perfect they are never satisfied."
- A 2024 substack post titled *"the 3-minute trick that changed my portfolio"* opens with the line: "share it with that designer friend who's been procrastinating on their portfolio for six months. We all have one."

**Implication for Closure:** the problem we're targeting is real, not hypothetical. We have multiple credible witnesses confirming the phenomenon. This is a strong foundation for slide 1's problem statement.

---

## Why designers procrastinate — pattern across sources

The reasons cluster into four groups, which map closely onto Max's talk's diagnosis:

### 1. Perfectionism and identity stakes
The portfolio is "the ultimate personal project" (Creative Bloq). Designers "approach it like a masterpiece" — and that pressure paralyzes them. This matches the talk's perfectionism / fear-of-failure driver almost word for word.

Stella Guan (2021) describes it as a feedback loop: *"The more you slack off on updating, the more overwhelming it will be when you do eventually update it."* Procrastination compounds.

A neurodivergent designer (gooddesigner substack) was direct: *"I'm a world-class procrastinator... To get things done I need a sense of urgency, novelty, accountability, and the right level of challenge."* Standard portfolio work has none of those.

### 2. The work-of-the-work problem
Tirso Gamboa (2020) describes the *operational* reason cleanly: *"in order to update your portfolio, you have to gather all the elements and then lay them out to fit the dimensions of your site."* You can't just upload final JPEGs. You have to re-document, re-cut, re-narrate. Portfolio updates are essentially a *second* production cycle on already-finished work.

This is exactly the gap Closure targets — but it's also where Closure's claim gets tested. The act of *capturing at the moment of completion* doesn't fully eliminate this work-of-the-work; it just *spreads it out*, replacing one painful documentation session with many small ones.

### 3. Case studies are the dreaded part
Multiple sources independently identify case study writing — not visual design, not layout, not assembly — as the specific bottleneck.

Van Schneider (DESK): *"Writing case studies might be the most dreaded part of building a design portfolio. After all the work and time it takes... who wants to actually sit down and EXPLAIN it all?"*

Tyler Design Institute (2020) cites Scott Kubie's *Writing for Designers*: *"writing is always hard, even for writers."* Designers are often visually trained and treat writing as a foreign skill — but case studies are essentially a writing genre disguised as a design artifact.

**Implication for Closure:** the 60-second voice capture sidesteps the "I'm not a writer" identity problem entirely. The AI does the writing; the designer just speaks. This is a real defensible angle, not a marketing claim.

### 4. The "not enough yet" trap
A counter-pattern surfaces in van Schneider's *"Why update my portfolio if I don't even like my work?"* — designers wait until they have work they're proud of, and never feel they do. The bar for "worth showing" never gets met.

This connects directly to your talk's perfectionism driver. Worth flagging because it suggests Closure's framing should *not* be "show off your best work" — it should be "capture what happened, regardless of whether it's good." Lowering the standard at point of capture is itself a design move.

## The architecture angle — different but adjacent

Less direct evidence of *procrastination* in architecture portfolio writing — architects' guides tend to be more pragmatic and instructional than emotional. But the same underlying problem appears:

- Architecture portfolios are required for licensure pathways, job applications, and academic admissions. Professional bodies like the Nigerian Institute of Architects (PPCE) require formal portfolio documentation as part of full membership.
- A Syracuse SOA workshop description (2024) describes portfolios as "site for project documentation, a structure for presentation, and a series of spaces in which to tell a story" — explicitly framing the problem as story-construction, not just image curation.
- Archisoup notes that portfolios "almost always [are] a requirement when starting a new program at a college or university" and ranges in length "between 20 to 40 pages" — substantially heavier than designer portfolios.

**Implication for Closure:** architects experience a *higher-stakes* version of the same problem — their portfolios are larger, more formal, and often required for credentialing. The act of capturing at the moment of completion may matter *more* for them, because the volume of work to reconstruct later is greater. But the emotional language ("I hate it") is less present in their writing — they treat it more as professional obligation than personal expression.

This gives us a possible angle for the persona work: **designers describe the pain emotionally; architects describe it operationally.** Closure could speak to both by acknowledging both.

## Cross-reference to perfectionism research

For Empathize-stage credibility, we found peer-reviewed and clinical perspectives that ground the perfectionism angle:

- *Beyond perfect? A case illustration of working with perfectionism using cognitive behavior therapy* (Limburg et al., 2017, cited in researchgate publication) describes perfectionism as a multidimensional construct strongly associated with procrastination. "Perfectionistic concerns" specifically link to depression, anxiety, and avoidance behaviors.
- The same source notes that perfectionists "tend to strive toward more difficult goals after achieving their standards" — the bar keeps moving. This is exactly the dynamic van Schneider describes in his "Why update my portfolio" piece.

**Implication for Closure:** we have clinical literature backing Max's talk's framing. If the jury pushes on whether procrastination-as-emotion-regulation is a real claim or pop psychology, we can cite peer-reviewed work that backs it.

## What the existing solutions look like

Worth knowing what's already out there in this space, because the brief warns against generic solutions:

- **Semplice, Carbonmade, Format** — portfolio platforms. Solve presentation, not the capture problem. Users still face the blank page.
- **Behance, Issuu** — hosting platforms with templates. Same gap as above.
- **The 3-minute Figma trick** (substack post) — a community-driven hack involving Easy Mockup plugin and Unsplash+. Tactical, not systemic.
- **Cohort programs** — gooddesigner's "Revitalise your design portfolio" 6-week collaborative cohort uses social accountability. Effective but high-friction.

**Implication for Closure:** there's no existing product that captures work *at the moment of completion* and turns it into case studies automatically. The closest analogues (cohorts, community accountability) target the same emotional problem but through different mechanisms. Our space is uncontested.

## Honest limitations of this research

A few things we should be aware of:

1. **It's secondary, not primary.** None of this replaces actual interviews with designers and architects we know. The primary interviews are still essential — they'll surface specific quotes and stories we can use directly in the presentation.

2. **The sample is self-selecting.** Bloggers who write about portfolio procrastination are unusually verbal and self-reflective. Less articulate procrastinators don't write blog posts; we won't hear from them this way.

3. **Most sources are designers, not architects.** The architecture-portfolio writing is more instructional and less confessional. We'll need primary interviews with architects to validate that the emotional pattern is the same.

4. **The sources skew toward freelancers and early-career designers.** Senior in-house designers and architects may experience this differently — they may have account managers or HR processes that force documentation. Worth probing.
