# User Journey Map — Current State
**Owner:** Both
**Status:** Draft
**Date:** 2026-05-30
**Type:** Current state (how the problem is lived today — no product intervention)
**Persona:** Mara Santos — product designer, 4 years experience, portfolio untouched for 18 months
**Sources:** Empathize_Synthesis.md, Survey_Round1_Synthesis.md, Research_Triangulation.md, Persona_Primary_Mara.md

---

## Scenario

Mara just shipped a three-month brand and UX project. The deliverable went out Thursday. This map follows what happens next — or more accurately, what doesn't.

---

## Journey

### Stage 1 — Ship

**What happens**
The project goes out. Final files delivered, client confirmation received. The work is done.

**Mara's actions**
Sends the final delivery email. Closes Figma. Moves to Slack to handle whatever is next.

**Thoughts**
> *"Finally. That took everything out of me."*

**Feelings**
Relief. Exhaustion. A faint sense of pride that hasn't fully landed yet.

**Pain points**
None yet. This is the window of highest memory and highest signal — she remembers every decision, every tension, every tradeoff. The window is open. She just isn't thinking about documentation.

---

### Stage 2 — The guilt surfaces (24–72 hours later)

**What happens**
The project is emotionally over. A quieter moment arrives — a Friday morning, a slow start to the week. The thought surfaces: *I should document that.*

**Mara's actions**
Opens a browser tab. Maybe Notion, maybe a blank Google Doc. Stares at it. Closes it.

**Thoughts**
> *"I know what I want to say. I just don't know how to start."*
> *"I should do this properly. But I don't have time to do it properly right now."*

**Feelings**
Low-grade guilt. Mild dread. The energy needed feels disproportionate to the moment.

**Pain points**
- The blank page stops her before she writes a word
- "Doing it properly" sets a bar she can't clear in the time available
- The memory is still there — but the motivation to access it isn't

---

### Stage 3 — The attempt (if it happens at all)

**What happens**
She decides to actually try. Opens a doc, maybe pulls up an old case study as a template.

**Mara's actions**
Tries to write an intro sentence. Rewrites it. Looks for screenshots in Figma. Tries to remember which version was the one she presented. Searches Slack for the feedback thread.

**Thoughts**
> *"What was the brief again? What was the actual problem I was solving?"*
> *"This is going to take hours. I don't have hours."*
> *"I don't even know if this project is worth showing."*

**Feelings**
Friction. The task feels harder than it should. Mild resentment — she did the work, why is the documentation this hard?

**Pain points**
- Writing in prose is a foreign skill — she described this fluently in client meetings but the blank doc stiffens her
- Assets are scattered across Figma versions, Slack threads, email chains
- The articulation gap: she knows what she decided, but not how to explain *why* to a stranger
- Time estimate balloons — what she thought would take 30 minutes is now clearly a 3-hour job

---

### Stage 4 — Deferral

**What happens**
The attempt stalls. She saves the draft (or doesn't). Tells herself she'll come back to it.

**Mara's actions**
Saves a half-finished doc somewhere. Or closes without saving. Moves on to the next project.

**Thoughts**
> *"I'll do this properly when things slow down."*
> *"Next project I'll document as I go."*

**Feelings**
Mild resignation. The guilt recedes. The intention persists — as a background hum.

**Pain points**
- "When things slow down" never arrives
- The memory continues to decay — by the time she returns, the nuance is gone
- Each deferred project makes the backlog feel more insurmountable

---

### Stage 5 — The backlog compounds

**What happens**
Weeks pass. Now there are two undocumented projects. Then three.

**Mara's actions**
Nothing — the backlog itself is now the blocker. Starting feels like catching up, not documenting.

**Thoughts**
> *"There's so much I haven't done. I don't even know where to start with the backlog."*
> *"My portfolio is so out of date it's almost embarrassing."*

**Feelings**
Heavier guilt. Avoidance. The portfolio becomes something she doesn't mention in conversations because it hurts slightly to think about.

**Pain points**
- The work-of-the-work problem compounds: each project requires re-entering a headspace that's fully closed
- Portfolio update now feels like a project in itself, not a maintenance task
- Identity gap widens — her actual skill level is years ahead of what her portfolio shows

---

### Stage 6 — The missed opportunity

**What happens**
A job opportunity appears. A client asks for a portfolio link. A speaking invitation arrives.

**Mara's actions**
Sends the old portfolio link with a caveat: *"It's a bit out of date, but..."* Or doesn't send it at all.

**Thoughts**
> *"I do good work. I just can't show it."*

**Feelings**
Frustration. A specific kind of regret — not about the work, but about failing to capture it while she could.

**Pain points**
- The opportunity cost is real and felt: work she's proud of is invisible
- The portfolio gap is now a professional liability, not just a to-do item
- The moment to capture the work is months gone. The memory is partial. It's too late to do it well.

## Emotion arc

```
Ship        Guilt       Attempt     Deferral    Backlog     Missed opp.
  |           |            |            |           |            |
Relief     Low dread    Friction   Resignation  Avoidance   Regret
  |           |            |            |           |            |
[HIGH]      [MED-]       [LOW]        [MED-]     [LOW]       [LOW-]
```

The emotional low points are **Stage 3** (friction of the attempt) and **Stage 6** (regret at the missed opportunity). The design intervention has the most leverage at **Stage 2** — where guilt surfaces but before the failed attempt sets in.

---

## Highest-leverage intervention window

**Stage 2, within 24–72 hours of shipping.**
Memory is still intact. The guilt is present but not yet compounded. The motivation exists — it just needs the path to be shorter than the alternative (the 3-hour proper attempt).

This is where Closure enters: not as a portfolio tool, but as a capture tool that works *before* the motivation evaporates.
