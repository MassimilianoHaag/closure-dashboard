## What to look for in the data

### The core hypotheses we're testing

| Hypothesis | Where it shows up | What confirms it |
|---|---|---|
| Most people don't document within 24 hours of shipping | Q5 | Majority select "No" or "Meant to but didn't" |
| Case study writing (the words) is the specific bottleneck — not asset gathering | Q7 | "Writing the words" or "blank page" outranks "gathering files" |
| The timing mechanic (capture in the moment) is meaningful | Q10 | Majority select "Yes — that's the point" |
| The concept has baseline desirability | Q8 | Majority select "I'd use this" or "interesting but doubts" over "not for me" |
| Perfectionism / "not good enough" is a real driver | Q6 open text | Look for "it wasn't impressive enough," "still needed polish," "embarrassed about the outcome" |

### What to read in the open-text responses (Q6 and Q9)

**Q6 — what got in the way:**
This is the primary qualitative asset. Read for:
- **Emotional language** ("exhausted," "didn't feel like it," "felt pointless") → confirms emotion-regulation thesis
- **Operational language** ("had to move on to the next thing," "couldn't find all the files") → confirms work-of-the-work driver
- **Identity language** ("I'm not a writer," "wouldn't know where to start") → confirms writing-as-bottleneck driver
- **Perfectionism language** ("wasn't good enough to show," "needed more polish") → confirms "not enough yet" trap
- **Surprises** — any reason pattern that doesn't fit the four-driver diagnosis. If something shows up repeatedly that we didn't predict, update the diagnosis.

**Q9 — concerns about the concept:**
This is your Q&A prep inventory. Read for:
- **Privacy / permanence concerns** ("what if I say something embarrassing")
- **Skepticism about AI output** ("will it actually sound like me")
- **Habit formation doubt** ("I'd still forget to do it")
- **The 'just ship' objection** ("I don't want to stop and do this right when I'm done")
- Any objection that shows up 3+ times is a slide or a FAQ answer

### Cross-tabs worth running
- Q3 × Q5: Do people who updated recently actually document in 24 hours? Or is Q3 disconnected from Q5 behavior?
- Q7 × Q1: Do architects cluster differently on the "most painful part" than designers?
- Q10 × Q5: Do people who didn't document in 24 hours say timing would matter? Or have they rationalized it away?

## Question design notes

**Q1 — multi-select on role:**
Architecture is explicitly listed because our secondary research surfaced it as a distinct audience. Multi-select matters because some respondents lead teams *and* do the work.

**Q3 — "be honest" prompt:**
Borrowed from the confessional framing in the secondary research. Signals that the embarrassing answer is expected. Without it, recency bias inflates reported update frequency.

**Q4 — anchor to last project, not general habits:**
This is the most important structural decision in the survey. General-behavior questions ("do you usually document?") produce socially desirable answers. Anchoring to a specific recent project produces behavioral truth. The secondary research shows bloggers only revealed the real pattern when describing specific experiences.

**Q5 — five-option behavioral question:**
"Meant to but didn't" is deliberately separated from "No." That specific option captures the most interesting psychological state — it's the gap between intent and action that Closure is designed to close. If that option dominates, it's the best possible validation of our framing.

**Q6 — open, anchored to specific project, explicit permission to be honest:**
This is the question that will generate the most valuable presentation material. The phrase "be honest" and the explicit examples ("I was exhausted," "it wasn't good enough") are permission structures — they normalize the answer we expect so respondents don't sanitize it.

**Q7 — "pick the worst one":**
Forces prioritization. Without that instruction, respondents check everything. The painful-writing options are listed before the asset-gathering option because we want to test whether writing is genuinely the top bottleneck — not prime it by putting it last.

**Q8 — concept description is specific, not aspirational:**
The paragraph describes the mechanism (60 seconds, AI sharpens, one follow-up, private gallery) rather than the benefit ("build your portfolio automatically"). Specific descriptions get more honest reactions. Benefit-first descriptions inflate positive responses.

**Q9 — "yeah, but..." framing:**
Invites skepticism explicitly. "What concerns do you have?" sounds clinical. "Yeah, but..." is how designers actually object to ideas in critique.

**Q10 — timing mechanic in isolation:**
This question tests one specific thing: whether the moment-of-completion timing is a feature or a constraint. The "My work doesn't have a clear done moment" option is there because we know architects and some product designers ship incrementally — if that option gets significant responses, it's a design implication for the capture trigger mechanic.

## Open questions before sending

1. **Response incentive?** The Slack post offers to share findings but no other incentive. If response rate matters (and it does), consider offering to DM results to everyone who responds.

2. **Should Q1 include "I'm not currently doing design work"?** Some respondents may be former designers or in career transition. Their answers would still be useful but may skew some responses.

3. **The concept description in Q8 doesn't mention the word "ritual."** That's deliberate — I didn't want to pre-load the framing. But if you want to test whether the word lands, you could add a version where it's explicitly a "closing ritual." I'd keep it out for v1.

4. **10 questions is at the limit for ~5 minutes on mobile.** Q6 and Q9 are both open-text, which slows people down. If response rate suffers, the first cut would be Q4 (time since last project) — it's useful but not load-bearing.

5. **Anonymous vs. named?** The post says anonymous. If you want the option to follow up with willing respondents for a 15-minute interview (which would be valuable), add an optional final field: "Would you be open to a 15-minute follow-up conversation? If yes, drop your name or Slack handle." This shouldn't compromise perceived anonymity if it's explicitly optional and final.
